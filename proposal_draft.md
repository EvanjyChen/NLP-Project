# Prompt-Based Readability-Controlled Text Simplification with Open-Weight LLMs

## Project Proposal — TSAR 2025 Shared Task

Text simplification rewrites a sentence so that it is easier to read while preserving its meaning, and it underpins accessibility tools for second-language learners, children, and readers with cognitive or visual impairments. We will participate in the TSAR 2025 shared task on readability-controlled text simplification, in which a system receives an English source sentence together with a target readability level (expressed on the CEFR scale, e.g., A2 or B1) and must produce a simplified sentence at that level. Our central research question is whether prompt-engineered open-weight large language models can match supervised baselines on simplification quality while also *controlling* the readability of their outputs. Concretely, we ask: (RQ1) how do zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought prompts compare on SARI and LENS against a supervised T5/MUSS baseline; (RQ2) how often do these systems land within the requested CEFR band; and (RQ3) does an iterative self-refine loop that re-prompts when the FKGL/CEFR target is missed close the gap between open-weight and proprietary models.

Our primary dataset is the TSAR 2025 shared-task corpus, which provides source sentences paired with CEFR-aligned simplifications and an official train/dev/test split. To support few-shot prompt construction and broader analysis we will also use ASSET (Alva-Manchego et al., 2020), which offers ten reference simplifications per source, and TurkCorpus (Xu et al., 2016) for SARI computation comparability with prior work. All three are publicly released under research licenses; no new annotation will be collected, in line with the course guidance to rely on existing datasets.

We will implement the prompting and self-refine pipeline ourselves on top of HuggingFace Transformers and vLLM for batched inference. The models we call are Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct, both quantized to 4-bit so they fit on a Colab T4, plus GPT-4o-mini through the OpenAI API as a paid-ceiling reference. As reproduced baselines we will re-run MUSS (Martin et al., 2022) and a T5-base model fine-tuned on WikiLarge. The hyperparameters we vary and report are sampling temperature (0.0–0.7), top-p (0.9–1.0), max new tokens (64), number of in-context examples (0, 3, 5), and the maximum number of refine iterations (0–3). Evaluation uses SARI as the primary metric, with BLEU, FKGL, BERTScore, and the learned LENS metric (Maddela et al., 2023) as complements; readability control is measured by the percentage of outputs whose predicted CEFR band matches the target, using a public CEFR classifier. A small human evaluation on 50 randomly sampled outputs, double-rated by group members on adequacy, fluency, and simplicity, will calibrate the automatic scores.

Our related-work review covers four threads: datasets and surveys (Alva-Manchego et al., 2020; Štajner, 2021), evaluation metrics (Xu et al., 2016 on SARI; Maddela et al., 2023 on LENS), controllable and unsupervised simplification (Martin et al., 2022 on MUSS; Agrawal and Carpuat, 2023 on controllable LLM generation), and recent LLM-based simplification (Kew et al., 2023 on the BLESS benchmark; Feng et al., 2023 on prompt-based simplification). Each paper is included because it either supplies a dataset or metric we use, motivates a baseline we re-implement, or directly anticipates the prompting question we are testing.

Beyond the libraries already mentioned, we will use EASSE for standardized SARI/BLEU/FKGL computation, PEFT in case a LoRA fine-tune of Llama-3 becomes necessary for an ablation, and Weights & Biases for experiment tracking. The deliverables include a results table reporting SARI/BLEU/FKGL/LENS for every model × prompting-strategy combination, a bar chart of CEFR-band hit rate, a scatter plot of SARI against inference cost in tokens per GPU-minute, and a qualitative table of five source sentences alongside each system's output for error analysis.

Our six-week timeline runs as follows. Week 1: download data, exploratory analysis, and replicate the T5/MUSS baseline so that the preliminary report is grounded in working numbers. Week 2: build the zero-shot and few-shot prompting pipeline on Llama-3.1-8B and Mistral-7B. Week 3: add chain-of-thought and self-refine variants and wire up the LENS and CEFR evaluators. Week 4: run the full evaluation grid and complete the human evaluation. Week 5: error analysis, ablations, and figure preparation. Week 6: write the final paper, design the poster, and record the eight-minute video.

The group has four members and the workload is split by component. As Member A, I will own the prompting pipeline and the self-refine loop, and I will produce the result tables and figures. Member B will handle data preparation, exploratory analysis, and the CEFR-classifier evaluation. Member C will reproduce the MUSS and T5-WikiLarge baselines and maintain the SARI/LENS evaluation harness. Member D will lead the related-work synthesis, coordinate the human evaluation, and run a final writing pass over the paper. Writing is shared throughout: each member drafts the section corresponding to the component they own, and we review each other's text in our weekly project meetings with the instructors.
